These four data points come from 15 years of monthly S&P 500 returns. Once you see them, you can never unsee them.

S&P 500 · November average
+1.7%

November has been the single strongest month in US equities for over 15 years. Post-election optimism, holiday spending forecasts, and institutional year-end positioning create a consistent tailwind that shows up year after year, regardless of the broader market regime.

✓  Positive in 70% of years
S&P 500 · September average
-0.7%

September is the only month to average a net loss in the S&P 500 across every decade of measurement. Back-from-summer institutional selling, mutual fund redemption cycles, and Q3 window dressing create a consistent and powerful headwind that has held up through bull and bear markets alike.

✗  Positive in only 42% of years
Bitcoin · October average
+21.4%

"Uptober" is Bitcoin's most reliable seasonal phenomenon, documented across 12 years of data. Halving cycle timing, institutional Q4 accumulation, and momentum from summer recovery combine into the single most consistent monthly surge of any major asset class. Positive in 9 of 12 years.

✓  Positive in 75% of years
Russell 2000 · January average
+2.8%

The January Effect is strongest in small caps. December's tax-loss selling sharply reverses as institutional and retail buyers return in January, creating the highest average monthly return of any major index in any month. The pattern has held in 68% of years over a 15-year period.

✓  Positive in 68% of years

Questions

Answered honestly.

Is seasonal data actually reliable? +
Seasonal patterns are statistical tendencies — not guarantees. Any individual year can and will deviate from the historical average. The value is probabilistic: when a month has a 70% win rate over 15 years, that's a meaningful edge when combined with other analysis. It's one input among several, not a crystal ball. We include explicit caveats in every analysis and never suggest acting on seasonal data alone.
Where does the data come from? +
Seasonal patterns are derived from historical monthly return data compiled from major financial data sources. The analysis reflects documented seasonal phenomena from academic finance research. We always show the specific observation count and time period so you know exactly what the data covers — no black boxes.
